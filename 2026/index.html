About the Workshop

A forum where geophysics meets technology

MNS4A is an international forum for geophysicists and technologists to discuss the most pressing challenges at the intersection of atmospheric modeling, observational networks, and scientific instrumentation — bridging fundamental science with real-world applications.

The workshop facilitates discussion on advancing real-time data acquisition, modeling techniques, and measurement systems — including applications that address both societal challenges and technological demands from the perspective of aeronomy and the atmosphere-ionosphere system.

Scientific Focus

Natural hazards & the ionosphere-atmosphere system

The primary scientific focus is on presenting advances and challenges in modeling and measuring the variability of the atmosphere-ionosphere system, with special emphasis on natural hazards and space weather phenomena.

Project IONO_DIET

Funded by FAPESP, this project investigates ionospheric variability and its links to meteorological conditions at the Earth's surface, troposphere, and near-space environments. The workshop is organized within its framework, aiming to produce a structured outline of sessions to be submitted to the scientific committee.

Workshop Sessions

Session structure

The workshop is organized into three core thematic sessions. Each session will have a responsible pair from the local committee, who will prepare a base text for review by the scientific committee.

SESSION 01

Modeling for Aeronomic Forecasting & Hazard Assessment

Contributions on modeling for forecasting applications, hazard characterization, emerging modeling techniques, improved data assimilation, and data–model integration. Modeling is central to aeronomy because it enables the quantitative testing of physical mechanisms that cannot be isolated from observations alone.

Models provide the framework needed to link neutral dynamics, ionospheric electrodynamics, plasma transport, composition, and conductivity across the lower thermosphere and ionosphere. Key features represented include Traveling Ionospheric Disturbances from natural hazards, Sq current systems, the equatorial electrojet, vertical E × B drifts, plasma density structures, sporadic E layers, and plasma irregularities.

Physics-based modeling Empirical models Data-driven methods Data assimilation TIDs & natural hazards Space weather Forecast capability
SESSION 02

Aeronomic Observational Networks & Instrumentation

The development of aeronomic research has advanced significantly through integrated observational networks combining ground-based and space-based systems, enabling simultaneous data acquisition across multiple spatial and temporal scales.

Ground-based instruments — including Fabry-Perot interferometers, radars, ionosondes, GNSS receivers, magnetometers, all-sky imagers, and riometers — offer continuous high-temporal-resolution measurements. This session presents major open scientific questions, with emphasis on identifying observational gaps and international efforts to address them.

Ground-based networks Satellite observations GNSS & GPS arrays Magnetometers Ionosondes Observational gaps Global coverage
SESSION 03

Scientific Instrumentation — Development, Deployment & Integration

Understanding atmospheric and space phenomena requires instrumentation capable of capturing processes across a wide range of spatiotemporal scales — from seconds to days, and from centimeters to thousands of kilometers. Ground-based and space-borne platforms, including rockets and satellites, are fundamental for investigating the coupled atmosphere-ionosphere-space environment system and its impacts on technological infrastructure and socioeconomic activities.

Ground-based instruments provide continuous, localized, and high-temporal-resolution observations, while space-borne instruments offer broader spatial and global coverage. Both passive and active observational techniques play important roles, each with their own constraints: passive optical systems are limited by illumination and weather conditions, while active instruments face challenges related to environmental interference, calibration, and maintenance costs.

This session encourages contributions addressing innovative approaches to instrument development, data acquisition, signal processing, and observational strategies — with particular emphasis on low-cost instrumentation, alternative hardware solutions, instrument intercomparison, and integrated observational techniques. Studies on nowcasting, data assimilation, and physics-based or numerical modeling efforts aimed at improving forecasting capability are also welcome.

Instrument development Low-cost solutions Signal processing Passive & active techniques Instrument intercomparison Data assimilation Nowcasting Space-borne platforms
Format & Participation

An inclusive scientific forum

MNS4A is designed to be a forum for researchers at all career stages. The organizing committee is committed to including early-career researchers in oral presentations, alongside invited senior scientists. The tone of the workshop is collaborative and open — this is not a workshop for the select few, but for anyone genuinely working to solve problems at the intersection of science, instrumentation, and societal need.

Early-career researchers are especially encouraged to participate and present their work. The scientific committee will actively seek to include emerging voices in oral sessions, recognizing that fresh perspectives are essential to the progress of the field.

Contributions are welcome in oral and poster format. The scientific committee will evaluate submissions and provide feedback on texts before final structuring of the program.
